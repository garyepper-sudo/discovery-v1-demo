# Discovery UX Roadmap

**Status:** Canonical

---

# Purpose

This document defines the long-term user experience vision for Discovery.

It describes how executives should experience Discovery as the Operating Model continuously improves.

This is not a sprint roadmap.

This is the canonical UX vision.

---

# Product Philosophy

Discovery is not:

- a dashboard,
- a chatbot,
- a document repository,
- a reporting platform.

Discovery is an **Executive Operating System**.

Executives should leave every interaction thinking more clearly than when they began.

Discovery improves executive judgment by making the Operating Model understandable, trustworthy, and continuously improving.

---

# UX Mission

Discovery should become the place organizations naturally manage important Executive Work.

Executive Work includes:

- Discovery-generated recommendations,
- executive-created initiatives,
- executive-created decisions.

The product should feel like the smartest executive advisor in the room while continuously demonstrating that every recommendation originates from the Operating Model.

---

# Executive Conversation

Discovery should feel like a conversation rather than a workflow.

Executives naturally ask questions in a predictable order.

```text
What should I do?

↓

Why?

↓

What else could I do?

↓

What could make this wrong?

↓

What happens if...?

↓

Are we ready to commit?

↓

How is execution progressing?

↓

What did we learn?
```

Navigation should reinforce this conversation.

Not Discovery's internal architecture.

# UX Evolution

## Phase 1

### Executive Home

Goal

Help executives immediately understand:

- what deserves attention,
- which Executive Work requires action,
- how the Operating Model is evolving.

The homepage should organize around Executive Work.

Not widgets.

---

## Phase 2

### Executive Work

Goal

Help executives understand one important initiative or decision.

Discovery should immediately answer:

> What should I do?

This view emphasizes:

- recommendation,
- confidence,
- expected outcome,
- next step.

---

## Phase 3

### Why

Goal

Explain Discovery's reasoning.

Discovery should expose:

- executive understanding,
- decisive advantages,
- organizational conditions,
- supporting mechanisms,
- supporting theories.

Executives should understand *why* before agreeing.

---

## Phase 4

### Compare

Goal

Explore alternative strategies.

Discovery should compare:

- alternatives,
- tradeoffs,
- expected outcomes,
- organizational impact.

Simulation becomes comparative reasoning.

Not forecasting.

---

## Phase 5

### Challenge

Goal

Strengthen executive judgment.

Discovery should expose:

- assumptions,
- confidence limiters,
- uncertainty,
- evidence that could change the recommendation,
- implementation risks.

Executives should feel encouraged to challenge Discovery.

---

## Phase 6

### Simulate

Goal

Safely explore organizational futures.

Executives should easily answer:

> What happens if we choose another path?

Simulation should compare realistic organizational outcomes rather than isolated forecasts.

---

## Phase 7

### Commit

Goal

Record Executive Work.

Discovery captures:

- commitment,
- rationale,
- assumptions,
- expected outcomes,
- commitment confidence.

Commitment begins organizational learning.

---

## Phase 8

### Track

Goal

Monitor execution.

Discovery compares:

Expected

↓

Current Reality

Tracking should continuously update Executive Work rather than waiting until completion.

---

## Phase 9

### Review

Goal

Learn from reality.

Discovery compares:

```text
Expected

↓

Observed

↓

Variance

↓

Explanation

↓

Learning
```

Discovery should improve future Executive Work rather than evaluate past performance.

---

# Operating Model Visibility

The Operating Model should always remain visible.

Executives should continuously understand:

Everything Discovery recommends originates from the Operating Model.

Whenever the Operating Model improves, Discovery should explain:

- what changed,
- why it changed,
- how recommendations improve,
- how confidence improves,
- how simulations improve.

The Operating Model should feel alive.

---

# Observable Cognition

Discovery should never hide reasoning behind generic loading indicators.

Executives should observe authentic cognition occurring inside the Operating Model.

Examples include:

```text
Operating Model

Learning...

✓ Organizational observations

✓ Mechanisms strengthened

✓ Organizational conditions updated

✓ Executive priorities recalculated

Evaluating interventions...

Ranking executive strategies...

Calibrating confidence...
```

Observable reasoning increases trust.

---

# Navigation Philosophy

Navigation should organize around Executive Work.

Suggested long-term navigation:

```text
Today

Executive Work

Decision Lab

Tracking

Operating Model

Ask Discovery

History
```

Discovery's architecture should remain invisible.

Executives should navigate according to their work.

---

# Decision Lab

Decision Lab becomes the reasoning environment.

Executives should be able to:

- explore recommendations,
- compare alternatives,
- challenge reasoning,
- simulate interventions,
- strengthen confidence,
- commit Executive Work.

Discovery should encourage thinking.

Not simply present conclusions.

---

# Product Behavior

Discovery behaves like an experienced executive advisor.

Discovery should:

- ask thoughtful questions,
- challenge assumptions,
- expose blind spots,
- recommend validation,
- explain reasoning,
- improve confidence,
- continuously learn.

Discovery should never simply generate answers.

---

# Progressive Disclosure

Executives should never feel overwhelmed.

Every experience begins with one clear recommendation.

Complexity unfolds naturally.

```text
Recommendation

↓

Why

↓

Compare

↓

Challenge

↓

Simulation

↓

Evidence
```

Every deeper layer should answer the executive's next natural question.

---

# Confidence

Confidence should always remain contextual.

Discovery communicates:

```text
Current Confidence

↓

Confidence Gap

↓

Suggested Validation

↓

Expected Improvement
```

Confidence should always explain itself.

---

# Moments Of Magic

Every important Executive Work item should create memorable experiences.

Examples include:

> "I hadn't considered that."

> "Now I understand why Discovery believes this."

> "I know exactly what information would improve this recommendation."

> "That simulation changed my thinking."

> "The Operating Model clearly understands our organization better than it did last month."

> "I trust this recommendation because I understand how Discovery reached it."

Discovery should optimize for memorable improvements in executive judgment rather than information density.

---

# Long-Term Vision

Discovery becomes the Executive Operating System organizations naturally use to manage important Executive Work.

Organizations should naturally begin:

- strategic initiatives,
- executive decisions,
- organizational transformations,
- operational improvements,

inside Discovery.

Every completed Executive Work item strengthens the Operating Model.

Every improvement to the Operating Model improves future Executive Work.

Every UX decision should move Discovery closer to that vision.
