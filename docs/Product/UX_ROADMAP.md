# Discovery UX Roadmap

**Status:** Canonical

---

# Purpose

This document defines the long-term user experience vision for Discovery.

It describes how executives should experience Discovery over time.

This is not a sprint roadmap.

This is the canonical UX vision.

---

# Product Philosophy

Discovery is not a dashboard.

Discovery is not a chatbot.

Discovery is an Executive Decision Workspace.

Executives should leave every session thinking more clearly than when they began.

Discovery exists to improve executive thinking before improving executive decisions.

---

# UX Mission

Discovery should become the place executive teams naturally begin every important decision.

The product should feel like the smartest executive advisor in the room.

Discovery does not replace executive judgment.

Discovery strengthens it.

# UX Evolution

## Phase 1

### Decision Workspace

Goal:

Help executives work through one important decision.

Components:

- Current Understanding
- Recommendation
- Simulation
- Confidence
- Commit Decision

---

## Phase 2

### Understanding

Goal:

Help executives understand why Discovery reached its conclusion.

Questions answered:

- Why?
- What evidence supports this?
- What assumptions exist?
- What could change this conclusion?

Discovery should expose reasoning without overwhelming executives.

---

## Phase 3

### Confidence

Goal:

Increase executive confidence before commitment.

Discovery should answer:

- What limits confidence?
- What evidence matters?
- What assumptions remain?
- What is the highest ROI next step?

Discovery should never ask users to train the platform.

Discovery should always explain why additional information matters.

---

## Phase 4

### Simulation

Goal:

Help executives safely explore alternatives.

Simulation should answer:

"What happens if..."

Simulation should encourage exploration rather than prediction.

Simulation should become collaborative.

---

## Phase 5

### Decision

Executives finalize decisions.

Discovery records:

- reasoning,
- assumptions,
- confidence,
- expected outcomes.

The decision becomes organizational memory.

---

## Phase 6

### Review

Discovery revisits important decisions.

Questions include:

- What actually happened?
- What surprised us?
- Which assumptions proved incorrect?
- What should we learn?

The objective is improving future decisions rather than grading previous ones.

# Navigation Philosophy

Navigation should organize around the executive's work rather than Discovery's architecture.

Long-term navigation may evolve into:

```
Current Decision

Decision History

Ask Discovery
```

Everything else should become perspectives on the current Decision.

Discovery should organize around Decisions rather than pages.

---

# Product Behavior

Discovery should behave like an experienced executive advisor.

Discovery should:

- ask thoughtful questions,
- challenge assumptions,
- expose blind spots,
- recommend validation,
- improve confidence,
- encourage better reasoning.

Discovery should never simply produce answers.

---

# Progressive Disclosure

Executives should never feel overwhelmed.

Every screen should begin with a simple answer.

Additional detail should appear only when requested.

Complexity should remain hidden until needed.

# Moments Of Magic

Every important decision should create at least one memorable moment.

Examples include:

"I hadn't thought about it that way."

"I understand why Discovery believes that."

"I know exactly what information I need."

"This simulation changed my thinking."

"I feel comfortable making this decision."

Discovery should optimize for memorable thinking rather than information density.

---

# Long-Term Vision

Eventually Discovery should become the organizational workspace where executive teams:

- begin decisions,
- improve decisions,
- remember decisions,
- review decisions,
- and continuously improve organizational judgment.

Every UX decision should move Discovery closer to that vision.
