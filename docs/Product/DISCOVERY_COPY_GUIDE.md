# Discovery Copy Guide

## 1. Status and Authority

**Status:** Canonical guidance for Discovery product and interface copy.

This guide is subordinate to the Product Canon, the Discovery Design Language,
and approved product specifications. It works with the UI System, Component
Library, Motion System, Experience Alpha specifications, and approved mockups.
It governs product copy, not broad marketing positioning. Future marketing
language may draw from it but does not override interface truthfulness.

Interface copy may not overstate the engine’s knowledge, access, governance,
learning, causality, progress, or capability. Approved visual designs may
resolve placement and hierarchy, but they may not weaken epistemic
qualification.

Labels used below:

- **Locked requirement** — required by existing canon.
- **Recommended default** — preferred until validation shows otherwise.
- **Illustrative example** — guidance, not fixture or production copy.
- **Unresolved decision** — requires product or content validation.

## 2. Voice Principles

### 2.1 Clear before clever

**Rationale:** Executive attention is scarce.

**Good:** “Approval boundaries differ across teams.”

**Bad:** “Your organization is navigating a decision-rights maze.”

### 2.2 Precise before impressive

**Rationale:** Trust grows from bounded claims, not grand language.

**Good:** “This pattern appears in three planning contexts.”

**Bad:** “Discovery uncovered a transformative truth.”

### 2.3 Concise before comprehensive

**Rationale:** Initial reading should carry only what judgment requires.

**Good:** “Ownership is unclear at cross-functional handoffs.”

**Bad:** “After comprehensively analyzing the available organizational
information, Discovery has determined that…”

### 2.4 Curious, not passive

**Rationale:** Unknowns should open a useful learning path.

**Good:** “Do teams interpret the escalation boundary differently?”

**Bad:** “More information may be needed.”

### 2.5 Confident enough to be useful

**Rationale:** Excessive qualification can make sound understanding unusable.

**Good:** “The strongest current explanation is inconsistent decision
ownership.”

**Bad:** “It is possible that there may perhaps be some ambiguity.”

### 2.6 Humble enough to remain credible

**Rationale:** A Living Understanding is never a final answer.

**Good:** “This explains the recurring delays, but not the regional
variation.”

**Bad:** “Discovery identified the definitive cause.”

### 2.7 Explain uncertainty

**Rationale:** A confidence label without a reason is not useful.

**Good:** “Moderate confidence: the pattern recurs, but evidence from customer
operations is limited.”

**Bad:** “Confidence: 68%.”

### 2.8 Name contradictions directly

**Rationale:** Contradictions deserve equal dignity with supporting evidence.

**Good:** “One region improved without changing approval layers.”

**Bad:** “There are a few minor exceptions.”

### 2.9 Avoid anthropomorphic theater

**Rationale:** Discovery can own product actions without pretending to think or
feel like a person.

**Good:** “Discovery is comparing the approved evidence.”

**Bad:** “I’m thinking deeply about your organization.”

### 2.10 Use executive language without management jargon

**Rationale:** The product supports judgment, not performance theater.

**Good:** “Decision ownership changes after escalation.”

**Bad:** “Align stakeholders around a synergistic operating cadence.”

### 2.11 Lead with meaning

**Rationale:** Operations and counts are supporting context.

**Good:** “Handoffs are delaying customer commitments.”

**Bad:** “Discovery processed 416 observations.”

### 2.12 Preserve user agency

**Rationale:** Discovery proposes and explains; people retain authority.

**Good:** “Review the learning plan before Discovery continues.”

**Bad:** “Discovery will proceed with the optimal sources.”

### 2.13 Describe bounded effects

**Rationale:** A contribution or action rarely changes everything.

**Good:** “This context adds an alternative explanation; the current
synthesis remains unchanged.”

**Bad:** “Your feedback fixed the model.”

### 2.14 Avoid artificial urgency

**Rationale:** Attention should follow consequence, not interface convention.

**Good:** “This contradiction could change the current explanation.”

**Bad:** “Urgent alert: review now!”

### 2.15 Reading burden is a product cost

**Rationale:** Every repeated or vague sentence competes with judgment.

**Good:** “What changed” followed by one dominant change.

**Bad:** Three headings that restate the same conclusion.

## 3. Canonical Terminology

| Term | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Living Understanding | The enduring, evolving user-facing object; use when continuity matters |
| Understanding | A specific current or historical organizational understanding |
| current synthesis | The concise best current explanation |
| learning | Meaningful improvement or qualification of an Understanding |
| evidence | Information represented by the canonical evidence layer; do not use for unsupported opinion |
| observation | A recurring or meaningful organizational observation when that distinction helps the user |
| contradiction | Evidence or meaning that conflicts with the current explanation |
| unknown | A specific knowledge boundary that matters |
| relationship | A qualified connection between Understandings or organizational meaning |
| confidence | Epistemic confidence in a stated object, always qualified |
| meaningful change | A consequential change in synthesis, confidence, unknown, contradiction, or relationship |
| follow | Ask Discovery to continue stewarding an Understanding |
| contribution | User-provided context, challenge, evidence, or interpretation |
| investigate | Deliberately reduce a named uncertainty |
| strengthen | Increase support for an explanation; state why |
| weaken | Reduce support; do not imply falsification unless supported |
| qualify | Narrow, bound, or add a material limitation |
| current explanation | The explanation best supported now |
| alternative explanation | A plausible competing interpretation |

Capitalize `Discovery`. Capitalize `Living Understanding` when naming the
canonical product object. Lowercase generic uses of “understanding.”

## 4. Terms to Avoid or Restrict

| Term | Guidance |
| --- | --- |
| insight | Restrict; prefer Understanding, learning, observation, or meaningful change |
| dashboard | Avoid for the Alpha journey; it implies monitoring and metric grids |
| AI-generated | Avoid in primary UI; expose provider provenance only where required |
| model output | Internal language; use current synthesis or Understanding |
| ingestion | Technical implementation term |
| pipeline | Technical implementation term |
| processing | Use only for a literal technical status outside normal UI |
| confidence score | Prefer qualified confidence; a number is not sufficient |
| final answer | Prohibited for a Living Understanding |
| complete | Restrict to bounded user actions, never an Understanding |
| solved | Prohibited for organizational understanding |
| root cause | Use only when causal support actually meets the product standard |
| monitor | Restrict; Following is stewardship, not monitoring |
| alert | Restrict to genuine safety/operational alerts outside this journey |
| optimization | Internal or explicitly approved decision context only |
| health score | Avoid; it compresses organizational meaning into a metric |
| truth | Restrict; use evidence, current explanation, or supported claim |
| verified | Use only when a defined verification occurred |
| proof | Prohibited unless the claim is literally proven |
| recommendation | Use only where the product context produces a canonical recommendation |

## 5. Naming the Product’s Agency

Acceptable patterns:

- “Discovery currently understands…”
- “Discovery found…”
- “Discovery is trying to understand…”
- “The strongest current explanation is…”
- “This would weaken the current explanation if…”
- “Discovery preserved this contradiction because…”
- “Discovery has not yet established…”

Avoid:

- “I think,” “I feel,” “I know,” or “I decided”;
- “The AI says”;
- “Discovery proved”;
- “Discovery believes you should…” outside a supported recommendation context.

First person is not used by the product voice in Experience Alpha. A quoted
human contribution may use the contributor’s first person. Future
conversation experiences may use a conversational first-person voice only if
canonical communication guidance explicitly authorizes it; that is outside
this guide.

## 6. Confidence Language

Qualitative language is the default:

| Level | Pattern |
| --- | --- |
| Not established | “Confidence is not yet established because…” |
| Low | “Low confidence: the explanation is plausible, but…” |
| Moderate | “Moderate confidence: the pattern recurs across…, while…” |
| High | “High confidence: independent evidence supports…, although…” |

Always name the object:

- synthesis: “Moderate confidence in the current synthesis…”
- evidence: “This evidence is direct but limited to one team.”
- causality: “The relationship is consistent; causality remains uncertain.”
- relationship: “This relationship is emerging across two contexts.”
- change: “Confidence weakened because the contradictory case expanded.”

Higher confidence never means better organizational performance. Confidence
movement uses strengthened, weakened, unchanged, or not yet established—not
improved, declined, succeeded, or failed.

## 7. Contradiction Language

Contradiction language is direct, calm, specific, and non-accusatory.

- New: “A new contradiction emerged: the region with the shortest cycle time
  retained the same approval layers.”
- Unresolved: “This contradiction remains unresolved because regional
  decision scope differs.”
- Weakens confidence: “This weakens confidence in approval count as the
  primary explanation.”
- Changes scope: “The contradiction narrows the explanation to
  cross-functional decisions.”
- Does not overturn: “This does not overturn the current synthesis, but it
  limits where the explanation applies.”

Do not call contradictions anomalies, noise, edge cases, exceptions, or
problems unless that meaning is supported.

## 8. Unknown Language

Every unknown states:

1. what is unknown;
2. why it matters;
3. what could clarify it.

Example:

> Discovery does not yet know whether escalation delay begins before or after
> customer commitments are made. That timing determines whether decision
> ownership or capacity is the stronger explanation. Comparing decision logs
> with commitment dates could clarify it.

Avoid “more data needed,” “insufficient information” without scope, or “TBD.”

## 9. Relationship Language

- Emerging: “An emerging relationship connects decision ambiguity with
  repeated escalation.”
- Meaningful: “These Understandings move together across three planning
  cycles.”
- Strong: “The relationship recurs across independent contexts.”
- Weakened: “The relationship weakened after evidence from the regional
  workflow was added.”
- Uncertain: “The relationship is visible, but direction and causality remain
  uncertain.”

Use relates to, coincides with, follows, or is associated with when causality
is not established. Use causes, drives, or leads to only with corresponding
causal support.

## 10. Learning Event Language

A Learning Event leads with what Discovery learned:

**Headline formulas**

- “[Meaning] became clearer.”
- “[Explanation] strengthened because [evidence].”
- “[Unknown] narrowed to [new boundary].”
- “A contradiction changed the scope of [synthesis].”
- “The relationship between [A] and [B] weakened.”

**Example**

Good: “Escalation delay begins before customer commitments are made.”

Supporting detail: “Decision logs place most delays at cross-functional
approval.”

Bad: “Processed 84 decision records.”

Counts and source operations may appear only as qualification or trace detail.

## 11. User Contribution Language

Contribution paths:

- Agreement: “This matches what I see.”
- Missing context: “Add context Discovery may be missing.”
- Alternative interpretation: “Offer another explanation.”
- Investigation: “Ask Discovery to investigate this.”
- Sensitive contribution: “This context may be sensitive. Review where it may
  be used before submitting.”
- Effect: “Discovery treated this as a provisional alternative explanation.”
- Limitation: “This contribution changes the scope, but does not resolve the
  contradiction.”

Avoid thumbs-up/down, feedback scoring, contributor authority ranking,
“executive override,” or “correcting the AI.” Agreement is not proof;
disagreement is not automatic correction.

## 12. Calls to Action

Use specific, sentence-case verbs:

- Ask
- Refine
- Examine
- Compare
- Add context
- Challenge
- Investigate
- Follow
- See what changed
- Continue learning
- Share evidence
- Pause learning
- Resume learning

Avoid Submit, Continue, Learn more, Click here, Generate, Run, or Process when
a more specific verb exists. Generic labels are allowed only when the action
truly has no more meaningful description. Labels describe the result, not the
control mechanics.

## 13. Empty States

An empty state names:

1. the current knowledge boundary;
2. why it exists, when known;
3. the useful next action.

Example:

> Discovery has not yet formed a relationship for this Understanding. Compare
> it with another followed Understanding to examine a possible connection.

Never use “No data,” an empty dash without explanation, or an invitation to
connect everything.

## 14. Loading and Waiting Copy

Avoid “Thinking…,” “AI is working…,” “Processing your data…,” and “Magic is
happening…”

Prefer:

- “Preparing the learning plan.”
- “Reviewing the selected source category.”
- “Preserving the current Understanding while this contribution is saved.”
- “This is taking longer than expected. Your input is preserved.”

Only name stages and sources that the system actually knows. Do not narrate
unsupported cognitive steps.

## 15. Error Language

Every error explains:

1. what happened;
2. what remains safe or stable;
3. whether the Understanding changed;
4. whether input was preserved;
5. what the user can do.

Example:

> Discovery could not save this contribution. The current Understanding did
> not change, and your text is still here. Try again.

Avoid blame, stack traces, provider details, opaque codes in primary copy, and
“Something went wrong” without recovery information.

## 16. Scene-Level Copy Guidance

The product-level scene sequence is Ask → Orient → Plan → Learn → Understand →
Respond → Follow → Return → Home. Detailed Alpha specifications retain Examine
and Challenge or Confirm as separate scenes. Until canon is reconciled,
Respond copy covers both examination and contribution pathways without
removing either behavior.

| Scene | Governing voice | Headline pattern | Primary action | Required qualification | Prohibited |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Ask | curious, sparse | “What would you like Discovery to understand?” | Ask | question can be refined | chatbot greeting, upload-first |
| Orient | interpretive, bounded | “Discovery understands your question as…” | Continue with this Understanding | included/excluded scope, low starting confidence | “We know what you mean” |
| Plan | deliberate, permission-aware | “To clarify this, Discovery would examine…” | Begin learning / approved label | reason, expected gain, limitation | connector marketplace language |
| Learn | observational, calm | “[Meaning] changed.” | Pause, continue, or examine | what changed and remains unknown | processed/scanned counts as headline |
| Understand | concise, useful | current synthesis | Examine why | confidence rationale, unknown, contradiction | final answer |
| Respond | respectful, specific | “How does this compare with what you see?” | Add context / Challenge / Share evidence | expected and accepted effect | feedback survey |
| Follow | quiet, prospective | “Keep learning about this Understanding?” | Follow | meaningful-change expectation, limitations | subscribe, notifications pitch |
| Return | editorial, consequential | “What changed while you were away” | See the most important change | why it changed, what remains unresolved | alert feed |
| Home | oriented, invitational | “Here is what Discovery learned” | context-specific highest-value action | stable items stay quiet | dashboard greeting plus metrics |

## 18. Progressive Disclosure

**Initial view**

- current synthesis;
- why it matters;
- qualified confidence;
- primary unknown;
- material contradiction;
- one primary action.

**Disclosure**

- confidence explanation;
- alternative explanation;
- supporting observations;
- source limitations;
- secondary meaningful changes.

**Evidence detail**

- evidence groups;
- observation context;
- contradiction support;
- access state.

**History**

- immutable prior synthesis;
- change cause;
- confidence movement;
- contribution effect.

**Source context**

- permitted provenance and scope;
- restrictions;
- no protected detail leakage.

Progressive disclosure never hides material uncertainty, contradiction, access
restriction, or the fact that causality is unestablished.

## 19. Tone by State

| State | Tone |
| --- | --- |
| Early orientation | tentative but useful; preserve original question |
| Low confidence | direct about the plausible explanation and its limits |
| Stable Understanding | calm; stability is meaningful, not completion |
| New contradiction | specific and non-alarmist |
| Increased uncertainty | treat as learning, explain why the boundary expanded |
| Meaningful change | lead with consequence, then cause |
| No meaningful change | concise; do not manufacture novelty |
| Restricted evidence | distinguish cannot disclose from has not learned |
| Error | factual, safe, recoverable |
| User disagreement | curious and bounded; describe how it will be treated |

## 20. Grammar and Style

- Use sentence case for headings, labels, and actions.
- Prefer full stops for explanatory copy; omit terminal punctuation on short
  control labels.
- Use contractions when they sound natural and do not reduce precision.
- Spell out zero through nine in prose; use numerals for measurements,
  versions, dates, and comparisons.
- Use unambiguous dates (“23 July 2026” or locale-appropriate fully stated
  dates); avoid “recently” when a date matters.
- Percentages use numerals and `%`, but only with rationale and adequate
  precision.
- Expand uncommon acronyms on first use.
- Capitalize Discovery and Living Understanding; do not capitalize ordinary
  object labels unnecessarily.
- Use em dashes sparingly; prefer two clear sentences.
- Use parentheses only for genuinely secondary context.
- Keep headings under roughly eight words where meaning permits.
- Keep initial-view paragraphs to two or three short sentences.
- Target plain executive language, approximately secondary-school reading
  level, without flattening necessary nuance.
- Address the user as “you” when describing an available action. Use “we” only
  for a real human/product team relationship, not product cognition.

## 22. Anti-Patterns

Discovery copy must not use:

1. anthropomorphic theater;
2. overconfidence;
3. unsupported causality;
4. generic AI language;
5. management jargon;
6. false urgency;
7. excessive enthusiasm;
8. exclamation marks in ordinary product states;
9. technical pipeline language;
10. passive ambiguity;
11. “more data needed” without a specific boundary;
12. confidence without explanation;
13. contradictions hidden in fine print;
14. uncertainty framed as failure;
15. long introductory copy before meaning;
16. duplicate headings;
17. generic CTA labels when a precise verb exists;
18. source counts as value;
19. “success” for a knowledge change;
20. “complete” for a Living Understanding;
21. agreement presented as truth;
22. disagreement presented as correction;
23. contributor authority scores;
24. invented personalization;
25. motivational language;
26. vague “insights”;
27. dashboard terminology;
28. alert terminology;
29. recommendation language in non-recommendation scenes;
30. qualification so excessive that the product becomes useless;
31. provider or model names in primary copy;
32. “real time” without verified behavior;
33. “continuous” when only a scripted fixture runs;
34. “private” or “secure” without a supported policy guarantee;
35. “anonymous” when identity may be inferable;
36. “we” used to imply human cognition;
37. labels that describe UI mechanics instead of outcomes;
38. counts without interpretation;
39. loaded terms such as “resistance” without evidence;
40. manufactured novelty on Return or Home.

## 23. Review Checklist

### Copy author

- Does the first sentence carry the meaning?
- Is every claim supported and bounded?
- Are confidence, contradiction, and unknown explicit?
- Is the action specific?
- Can any sentence be removed?

### Component author

- Does the component preserve canonical labels?
- Is dynamic copy supplied by an explicit view model?
- Are loading, empty, restricted, and error variants truthful?
- Does truncation preserve qualification?

### Scene author

- Does the copy answer the scene’s governing question?
- Is there one primary narrative and action?
- Does progressive disclosure avoid duplication?
- Are prior and current states distinguishable?

### Reviewer

- Is fact separated from inference, possibility, and recommendation?
- Is causal language supported?
- Does copy preserve user authority?
- Could restricted or absent information be confused?
- Does the copy resemble a chatbot, dashboard, or AI theater?

### Codex

- Use repository terminology exactly.
- Do not invent capability or validation claims.
- Preserve fixture wording when instructed.
- Record authority conflicts rather than silently resolving them.
- Do not turn illustrative examples into production contracts.

## 24. Open Decisions

1. Whether numeric confidence appears in the default experience remains
   unresolved.
2. The final phrase for the approved Plan primary action remains a
   high-fidelity design decision.
3. The canonical scene naming conflict between Examine /
   Challenge-or-Confirm and Respond requires reconciliation.
4. Whether Return is a state within Home or a distinct route remains
   unresolved.
5. The minimum visible evidence language needed for trust at each confidence
   level requires user testing.
6. Language distinguishing “not learned” from “cannot disclose” must be tested
   for privacy side channels before canonizing phrase variants.
7. Localization tone and terminology require later research; no localization
   infrastructure is implied here.

## 25. Definition of Done

Discovery interface copy is complete when:

- it answers the active scene question quickly;
- it leads with meaning and reduces reading burden;
- every knowledge claim is precise and supported;
- confidence includes its object, rationale, and limitation;
- contradictions and unknowns are visible and useful;
- causal language is qualified;
- user agency and contribution boundaries are explicit;
- loading and error language is truthful;
- progressive disclosure does not hide material uncertainty;
- terminology matches the Product Canon and component contracts;
- accessibility labels preserve the same meaning;
- no copy implies unsupported intelligence, governance, privacy, progress, or
  production behavior.
