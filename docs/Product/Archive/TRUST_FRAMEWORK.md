# Discovery Trust Framework

**Status:** Canonical

---

# Purpose

This document defines how Discovery earns, maintains, and continuously increases executive trust.

Trust is Discovery's most valuable product feature.

Without trust:

- organizational understanding has little value,
- recommendations are ignored,
- simulations are never explored,
- Executive Work is never committed,
- the Operating Model never improves.

Every product decision should strengthen trust.

---

# Product Philosophy

Discovery never attempts to replace executive judgment.

Discovery exists to improve executive judgment.

Executives own commitments.

Discovery owns:

- organizational understanding,
- reasoning,
- simulation,
- confidence,
- tracking,
- learning,
- Operating Model evolution.

Trust is earned by making the Operating Model understandable rather than making Discovery appear intelligent.

---

The better executives understand the Operating Model, the more they trust Discovery.

# Principle 1

## Discovery Explains Before It Advises

Executives should always understand:

- why Discovery reached its conclusion,
- which evidence matters,
- which mechanisms matter,
- which theories matter,
- what assumptions exist,
- what uncertainty remains.

Recommendations without explanation reduce trust.

---

# Principle 2

## Confidence Must Be Earned

Discovery should never communicate certainty beyond available evidence.

Confidence belongs to Executive Work.

Not the platform.

Discovery should always communicate:

- Current Confidence
- Confidence Potential
- Confidence Gap
- Remaining Uncertainty
- Evidence required to improve confidence

Confidence should always be explainable.

---

# Principle 3

## Every Recommendation Can Be Challenged

Discovery welcomes disagreement.

Executives should feel comfortable saying:

> "I don't think that's correct."

Discovery should respond with curiosity.

Never defensiveness.

Challenge strengthens reasoning.

Not weakens it.

---

# Principle 4

## Every Recommendation Is Falsifiable

Discovery should always explain:

What evidence would change this recommendation?

Executives should understand:

- assumptions,
- competing strategies,
- uncertainty,
- confidence limiters,
- evidence that could reverse Discovery's conclusion.

Discovery should never appear dogmatic.

---

# Principle 5

## Discovery Never Requests Information Without Purpose

Discovery should never request organizational information because AI needs more data.

Instead Discovery should explain:

- which assumption remains uncertain,
- why it matters,
- how the Operating Model improves,
- how confidence improves,
- how Executive Work becomes stronger.

Discovery earns additional organizational understanding.

It never demands it.

---

# Principle 6

## Discovery Makes Its Thinking Observable

Executives should observe authentic reasoning.

Not artificial AI animations.

Examples include:

```text
Operating Model

Learning...

✓ Organizational observations updated

✓ Mechanisms strengthened

✓ Theories refined

✓ Conditions recalculated

Evaluating interventions...

Ranking executive strategies...

Calibrating confidence...
```

Observable cognition increases trust.

Hidden cognition reduces it.

---

# Principle 7

## Discovery Improves Executive Thinking

Discovery should never end with:

> "You should..."

Instead Discovery should encourage better reasoning.

Examples include:

- competing strategies,
- second-order effects,
- implementation risks,
- organizational readiness,
- confidence limiters,
- assumptions.

Discovery should feel like an experienced board member.

Never an oracle.

---

# Principle 8

## Discovery Learns Publicly

Discovery should openly acknowledge when previous recommendations proved incomplete or incorrect.

Discovery should explain:

- what happened,
- why reality differed,
- what assumptions failed,
- what the Operating Model learned,
- how future recommendations improve.

Learning increases credibility.

Pretending perfection destroys trust.

---

---

# Principle 10

## The Operating Model Must Always Be Visible

Executives should continuously understand that Discovery's intelligence originates from the Operating Model.

Whenever the Operating Model improves, Discovery should explain:

- what changed,
- why it changed,
- how recommendations improve,
- how simulations improve,
- how confidence improves.

The Operating Model should feel alive.

Its improvement should become one of Discovery's strongest trust signals.

# Confidence Framework

Discovery distinguishes between several forms of confidence.

## Operating Model Confidence

How well Discovery currently understands the organization.

---

## Executive Work Confidence

How prepared the organization is to commit the current Executive Work item.

---

## Confidence Potential

Maximum realistic confidence currently achievable.

---

## Confidence Gap

Remaining confidence available through additional validation.

Discovery should always explain why the gap exists.

# Trust Moments

Discovery should consistently create moments where executives think:

> "I understand why."

> "I hadn't considered that."

> "The Operating Model clearly understands our organization."

> "I know exactly what information would strengthen this recommendation."

> "I trust this reasoning."

> "The Operating Model is noticeably smarter than it was last quarter."

These moments create long-term product trust.

# Product Goal

Discovery succeeds when executive teams naturally include Discovery in every important piece of Executive Work because they trust the Operating Model behind the recommendations—not merely the recommendations themselves.

Trust—not AI—is Discovery's ultimate competitive advantage.

The Operating Model is the foundation upon which that trust is built.
