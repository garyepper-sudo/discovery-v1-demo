# Atlas Evaluation Loop

**Status:** Canonical

---

# Purpose

The Atlas Evaluation Loop is the canonical process by which Discovery determines whether its understanding accurately anticipated future organizational behavior.

Its purpose is to transform organizational prediction into organizational learning.

Reasoning alone is insufficient.

Discovery must continuously evaluate whether its expectations match reality and adapt its cognition accordingly.

The Evaluation Loop therefore closes Discovery's cognitive feedback cycle.

---

# Core Principle

Discovery should never simply make predictions.

Discovery should determine:

* whether the prediction was correct,
* why it was correct,
* why it was incorrect,
* what assumptions proved invalid,
* how confidence should change,
* how beliefs should evolve,
* and what should be investigated next.

Every prediction must eventually become learning.

# Phase 1 — Prediction Matching

Atlas identifies predictions whose evaluation window has arrived.

Predictions are matched against subsequent organizational evidence.

Each prediction should reference:

* originating organizational conditions,
* supporting beliefs,
* supporting theories,
* assumptions,
* expected organizational changes,
* expected time horizon.

Only evidence occurring after prediction creation may be used.

---

# Phase 2 — Outcome Classification

Each prediction is assigned one outcome.

## Correct

Observed organizational behavior matches the predicted outcome.

---

## Partially Correct

Some expected effects occurred.

Other expected effects did not.

---

## Incorrect

Observed organizational behavior contradicts the prediction.

---

## Unresolved

Insufficient evidence currently exists.

Prediction remains active.

---

## Invalidated by Intervention

An executive action fundamentally changed organizational conditions.

The prediction should not be treated as incorrect.

Instead Discovery should recognize that organizational intervention altered the expected trajectory.

---

# Phase 3 — Causal Evaluation

Atlas evaluates whether Discovery understood *why* the organization evolved.

Prediction accuracy alone is insufficient.

Discovery should distinguish:

* causal correctness,
* correlation,
* coincidence,
* and incomplete explanation.

Questions include:

* Were the correct mechanisms identified?
* Were the dominant theories appropriate?
* Did Discovery overestimate one mechanism?
* Did Discovery ignore competing explanations?
* Did unexpected mechanisms emerge?

---

# Phase 4 — Belief Revision

Beliefs evolve according to evaluated outcomes.

## Confirmed Beliefs

Repeatedly successful predictions strengthen supporting beliefs.

Confidence gradually increases.

---

## Weakened Beliefs

Incorrect predictions reduce belief confidence.

Supporting evidence should be reconsidered.

---

## Replaced Beliefs

When contradictory evidence consistently appears, obsolete beliefs should be retired and replaced.

Discovery should not preserve unsupported organizational beliefs indefinitely.

---

## Emerging Beliefs

Previously weak explanations may become dominant as new evidence accumulates.

Atlas should encourage discovery of stronger explanations rather than defending existing conclusions.

---

# Phase 5 — Confidence Calibration

Confidence represents calibrated belief rather than certainty.

Confidence should change gradually.

## Increase Confidence

When:

* predictions repeatedly succeed,
* supporting evidence expands,
* competing theories weaken,
* mechanisms remain stable.

---

## Decrease Confidence

When:

* predictions fail,
* contradictory evidence accumulates,
* competing theories strengthen,
* assumptions prove invalid.

---

## Maintain Confidence

When evidence remains mixed.

Discovery should avoid unnecessary oscillation.

---

# Phase 6 — Learning Profile Update

Learning metrics should reflect Discovery's adaptive performance.

Examples include:

* prediction accuracy
* confidence calibration quality
* belief adaptation rate
* theory stability
* organizational memory growth
* investigation effectiveness

Learning Profile metrics should describe how Discovery itself is improving.

---

# Prediction Evaluation Record

Every evaluated prediction produces a persistent evaluation object.

Conceptually:

```text
Prediction

↓

Observed Outcome

↓

Evaluation

↓

Learning

↓

Updated Understanding
```

Each evaluation should capture:

* prediction identifier
* prediction date
* evaluation date
* predicted outcome
* observed outcome
* evaluation status
* confidence before evaluation
* confidence after evaluation
* supporting evidence
* contradictory evidence
* causal explanation
* executive implications
* resulting belief updates

Prediction evaluations become part of Discovery's persistent organizational memory.

---

# Executive Intervention Handling

Executive actions may intentionally alter organizational trajectories.

Examples include:

* organizational restructuring
* leadership replacement
* process redesign
* staffing changes
* operating model improvements

If intervention changes the organization before the prediction window completes:

* Discovery should identify intervention effects.
* Discovery should determine whether the prediction would likely have succeeded without intervention.
* Prediction quality should be evaluated independently from intervention success.

Discovery should distinguish:

"I predicted incorrectly."

from

"The organization changed because leadership intervened."

---

# Falsification

Atlas actively searches for evidence that weakens current understanding.

Discovery should value falsifying evidence.

A theory becomes stronger by surviving attempts to disprove it.

Every prediction should therefore include:

* expected confirming evidence,
* expected contradictory evidence,
* explicit falsifying signals.

---

# Evaluation Objectives

Atlas measures whether Discovery becomes a better organizational learner.

Success includes:

* increasing prediction accuracy,
* improving confidence calibration,
* reducing unsupported beliefs,
* improving executive recommendations,
* discovering stronger organizational explanations,
* adapting to contradictory evidence,
* improving future investigations.

Learning should be observable across multiple simulation periods.

Simulation creates experience.

Together they form Discovery's adaptive learning system.
